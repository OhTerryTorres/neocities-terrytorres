<\/p>\n

and takes place in his perception of the United States,<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

So ever since Earthbound came out on the Wii U's Virtual Console - which is an incredible concession on Nintendo's part, but since none of the music in the game has been removed, clearly Nintendo of America's insistence that the game could not be re-released due to copyrighted song samples was a huuuuge lie - everybody's jumpin' back onto the bandwagon that I've been carting all on my lonesome.<\/p>\n

Amongst all this, though, some good reading has appeared. Not only did Nintendo give Earthbound its own site and sweet promotional video<\/a>, there's also a brief essay (no, more like poem) from Itoi himself<\/a>, reflecting on Earthbound and its purpose.<\/p>\n

There's also more than one interview online with Marcus Lindblom, the man who almost single-handedly localized Earthbound for the West<\/a>.<\/p>\n

These things made me realize that all of my favorite games, to some degree, are like Earthbound; in that they not only make great use of the interactive and long-form nature of the medium, but also are unafraid to include the strange, personal things that other single-minded, artist-driven mediums have been using for many years.<\/p>\n

With Earthbound's re-release, there is a new layer of purpose to my writing. Now that people are actually playing it, we can actually discuss its importance - what it got right, what others have ignored, and who has been paying attention.<\/p>\n
